1914 - Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand


In June 1914, Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria was assassinated in Sarajevo, a Serbian nationalist an event that would trigger the outbreak of World War I.

Chain Reaction That Led to War:

  1. Austrian-Hungary blamed Serbia and declared war.
  2. Russia supported Serbia and mobilized its army.
  3. Germany supported Austria-Hungary and declared war on Russia.
  4. Germany declared war on France.
  5. Germany invaded Belgium to attack France.
  6. Britain entered the war to defend Belgium.
1914

US declares Neutrality



President Woodrow Wilson declares neutrality because:-
He wanted to avoid European conflicts
There was strong isolationist sentiment among citizens
There was a diverse immigrant population with ties to both sides



May 7, 1915

German U-boat sinks British ocean liner Lusitania.



Resulted in death of 128 Americans
This caused outrage over loss of American lives, Wilson demands Germany cease unrestricted submarine warfare.



1917

US declares War



In January 1917, Germany resumes attacking all ships, including neutral ones which caused several U.S. merchant ships to sink
Germany secretly proposes alliance with Mexico and Japan against the U.S. in the Zimmermann Telegram

Promising Mexico U.S. territories (Texas, Arizona, New Mexico) which was intercepted by Britain and revealed to the U.S. and angered U.S.

April 6, 1917: U.S. officially declares war on Germany:

Formation of the American Expeditionary Forces (AEF) under General John J. Pershing.
U.S. insists on independent command, not merging fully with British or French forces.

The world must be made safe for democracy - President Woodrow Wilson



1917 - 1918

U.S. Mobilization



Selective Service Act (1917): Military draft begins, Over 4 million Americans serve.

Massive industrial production to supply Allies with:
Weapons
Ships
Food supplies
which made U.S. economy fully converted to wartime production



Battle of Cantigny (May 28, 1918)

First major U.S. offensive victory



U.S. 1st Division captures the village of Cantigny, France from Germany. Successfully holding position against multiple counterattacks which help raised morale of the soldiers and achieve their first every victory.



Battle of Château-Thierry (May–June 1918)

Preventing German advance toward Paris



U.S. Special Forces play a crucial role in the battle, utilizing their training and expertise to hold key positions against the German advance, which then helped to stop the German offensive toward Paris.
This helped increase the reputation of U.S. troops among Allies. And would mark this their first defensive success in the war.



Battle of Belleau Wood (June 1–26, 1918)

Legendary U.S. Marine Corps victory



U.S. Marines launched a counterattack against German forces in the Belleau Wood area of France. Despite heavy casualties, they successfully captured key positions and held them against fierce counterattacks. This battle is often regarded as a turning point for the U.S. Marine Corps and helped establish their reputation as elite shock troops.
Germans reportedly nickname Marines “Teufelshunde” (Devil Dogs).



Battle of the Marne (July 15 – August 6, 1918)

Second battle of Marne lead to the end of the war


Germans wanted to defeat the allies without American involvement, but the U.S. forces played a crucial role in the Allied counteroffensive, helping to repel the last major German offensive of the war.
Leading to a decisive victory for the Allies, marking the end of the war.



Meuse–Argonne Offensive (Sept 26 – Nov 11, 1918)

Largest and deadliest U.S. military operation up to that time



U.S. forces launched a massive offensive against German positions in the Meuse-Argonne region of France. This operation involved 1.2 million American troops and aimed to break through German defenses.

Notable personalities involved:

- Alvin York: One of the most decorated American soldiers of WWI, York single-handedly captured 132 German soldiers during the offensive, earning the Medal of Honor.
- General John J. Pershing: Commander of the American Expeditionary Forces (AEF), Pershing played a key role in planning and executing the offensive.



Treaty of Versailles (June 28, 1919)

Peace treaty that ended WWI


The Treaty of Versailles officially ended World War I.
The treaty imposed heavy reparations and territorial losses on Germany, leading to significant political and economic consequences.
It also led to the creation of the League of Nations, an international organization aimed at maintaining peace and preventing future conflicts. But the U.S. did not join the treaty and went back to isolationist policies.



In the 1930s


The war created economic collapse and public resentment in Germany. Directly contributed to the rise of extreme nationalism.

This led to the rise of Adolf Hitler, Benito Mussolini and Imperial Japan
Expansionist Policies (1930s):

  1. Japan expands into Asia.
  2. Italy invades Ethiopia.
  3. Germany reoccupies the Rhineland.
  4. Germany annexes Austria.
  5. Germany demands Sudetenland from Czechoslovakia.
  6. Germany invades Poland.
  7. Italy invades France.
  8. Germany invades the Soviet Union.
  9. International response is weak.



1939

German Invasion of Poland



The German invasion of Poland marked the beginning of World War II in Europe. This operation involved a rapid and brutal assault by German forces, utilizing blitzkrieg tactics to overwhelm Polish defenses.

Notable personalities involved:

- Adolf Hitler: The Führer of Nazi Germany, Hitler orchestrated the invasion as part of his expansionist agenda.
- General Heinz Guderian: A key proponent of blitzkrieg tactics, Guderian played a significant role in the rapid advance of German forces into Poland.
U.S. response was initially neutral, but the invasion set the stage for future involvement in the war.



1940 – 1941

Supporting the Allies


Lend-Lease Act (1941): U.S. supplies weapons, food, ships to Britain, USSR, and others. U.S. Navy escorts Allied ships in the Atlantic. America becomes the “Arsenal of Democracy.”



Pearl Harbor - December 7, 1941

Japan attacks Pearl Harbor, Hawaii.



Japan attacks U.S. by deploying bombs from thier Mitsubushi zeros and various other bombers through the pacific front and deal major deaths accounting to thousands.
The following day U.S. declares war on Japan followed by Germany and italy declaring war on U.S. thus ending isolationist policies



Two Front War


The United States fought a two-front war during World War II, engaging in major military operations in both the European and Pacific Theaters.

European Theater:
Actively engaging Germany and Italy in Europe and North Africa.

Pacific Theater:
Fighting against Japan across the vast Pacific Ocean, including island-hopping campaigns and major naval battles



Pacific Theater




Battle of Midway (June 4 – 7, 1942)

All out war in the Pacific


U.S. navy destroys 4 japanese fleet carriers and 1 heavy cruisers
This shifted the entire balance of power towards U.S.



Guadalcanal Campaign (Aug 1942 – Feb 1943)

First major U.S. offensive in ground



U.S. marines went face to face to securing Henderson field from Japan in the vast jungles of the south-east



Island-Hopping Strategy (1943–1945)

Gaining back the islands from the Japanese


U.S. captures key islands while bypassing others resulting in cuting off Japanese supply lines.



Battle of Leyte Gulf (October 1944)

Complete control of the Pacific


The Battle of Leyte Gulf was the largest naval battle in history and marked the effective destruction of the Japanese Navy. After this battle, Japan lost the ability to conduct major naval operations.



Battle of Iwo Jima (February–March 1945)

A Costly Step Toward Japan


The Battle of Iwo Jima was one of the bloodiest battles of the Pacific War. U.S. Marines captured the island after intense fighting.

The iconic flag-raising on Mount Suribachi became a lasting symbol of American sacrifice.



Battle of Okinawa (April–June 1945)

The Final Barrier Before Japan


The Battle of Okinawa was the last major battle before a planned invasion of Japan. Japanese forces used fierce resistance and kamikaze attacks.

Casualties were extremely high on both sides.

Achievement: Positioned U.S. forces close to the Japanese mainland.



Atomic Bombings (August 1945)

The End of the War


On August 6, 1945, the United States dropped an atomic bomb on Hiroshima. A second atomic bomb was dropped on Nagasaki on August 9, 1945.

Japan announced its surrender on August 15, 1945. Brought World War II to a decisive end.



European Theater




Operation Torch (November 1942)

America Enters the European Theater


Operation Torch marked the first major U.S. ground combat operations in Europe. American and British forces landed in North Africa to challenge Axis control.



Invasion of Italy (1943)

Breaking the Axis Powers


Allied forces invaded Italy, leading to the surrender of the Italian government. Despite this, German forces continued fighting in the region, in the end it removed Italy from the Axis alliance.



D-Day – Normandy Invasion (June 6, 1944)

The Opening of the Western Front


D-Day was the largest amphibious invasion in history. U.S. forces landed at Omaha and Utah beaches under heavy fire.



Liberation of France (1944)

Freedom Restored


Following the Normandy landings, Allied forces pushed across France. Paris was liberated after years of German occupation.



Battle of the Bulge (Dec 1944 – Jan 1945)

Germany’s Last Stand


Germany launched a surprise winter offensive in Belgium. U.S. forces held their positions despite harsh conditions which crushed Germany’s final major offensive.



Victory in Europe (May 7, 1945)

The Defeat of Nazi Germany


U.S. forces crossed the Rhine River and advanced into Germany. American troops met Soviet forces at the Elbe River.

Germany surrendered unconditionally on May 7, 1945. Ending World War II in Europe.



End of World War II

The conclusion of World War II was marked by the unconditional surrender of the Axis powers. In Europe, this was achieved with the fall of Berlin and the surrender of Germany on May 7, 1945. In the Pacific, the war ended following the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, leading to Japan's surrender on September 2, 1945.
